2017 Election for National House of Representatives

Using data put together by Steve Riffe at data.world for the 2017 Election for the House of Representatives, I have built two statistical models for predicting whether a district will elect a Democratic or Republican candidate based on its ethno-racial demographics. Riffe's demographic data was taken from the US Census Bureau's 2013 estimates . The Census Bureau's estimates use the following designations for the ethno-racial groups:

  • Hispanic
  • White
  • Black
  • Native American
  • Asian
  • Pacific Islander
  • Other
  • Multiple Races


Rather than simply use the raw data, I engineered new features from each ethno-racial category and the total estimated population of each district so that each category was represented as a percentage of population. Using the same principle, I also created a voter turnout feature; however, due to Riffe's lack of clarity in creating a data dictionary for this dataset, it is not clear as to whether this should interpreted as voter turnout or the turnout for the victorious candidate. Given the differences in magnitude between the high percentage values, i.e. the majority, Whites, and the sizeable minorities, Blacks and Hispanics, and smaller minories such as Asians and Pacific Islanders, I followed best practices and standardized the data with StandardScaler in a SciKitLearn pipeline for each model.

Seeing that the question called for a classification model, I chose SciKitLearn's LogisticRegression and XGBoost's XGBoostClassifier. Since the former is a linear model and the latter is tree-based, I decided that this would be a good opporotunity to outline some of the key differences between the two model types. I have also used a train, val, test split with the former and cross validation for the latter and will outline some of the key differences between the two approaches below.

Each case was measured against a mode-baseline in which each distric was predicted to elect a Republican representative. The accuracy score was chosen as the error-metric by which each model was compared with the baseline and against each other.

The mode-baseline for 2017 netted an accuracy score of 54.73%
